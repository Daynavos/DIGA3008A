Blog 1: GitHub and Goals

My experience setting up GitHub and my goals for this course.

17 February 2025

Setting up GitHub was a straightforward process. I found it fascinating to discover how simple it is to publish a web page. This demystified the process of creating websites, for which I am grateful. I read “As We May Think at 65,” along with articles about the dot-com bubble and Web 2.0. These three readings complement each other well. One key takeaway for me was the personal aspect explored in both “Think 65” and “Web 2.0.” Bush envisioned access to information mimicking the human brain, aiming for a dynamic thought process rather than the hierarchical structure of alphabetized lists. I believe Hypermedia is the closest we've come to replicating the human process of jumping from one thought to another. The personal aspect, however, is what intrigues me the most. Web 2.0 focuses on making the web an interactive and personal experience. While this has many benefits, a concerning truth is that much of this personalization is profit-driven. Personalized ads are a prime example, though the implications go far beyond this. Main Concerns:

  1. Ecosystems of Conformity: Within the web, ecosystems are being created where people are shown only what they want to see and are reinforced in their beliefs. This could allow harmful views to thrive unchecked.
  2. Capitalist Overtones: The dot-com bubble illustrates how much of the web is driven by publicly traded companies. Their profits depend on continuous growth, which drives tactics aimed at capturing attention and increasing time spent online. Such practices can foster addiction and unsafe habits.

As a result, the web has become an overwhelming space. While Bush's vision of dynamic information retrieval has been achieved, it is often overshadowed by the noise created by profit-driven motives. This constant growth desired by tech companies seems unsustainable, and the dot-com bubble at the turn of the century signals the risks of unchecked expansion.

Blog 2: Laws of Media

A reflection on Moulthrop's "Hypertext and the Laws of Media".

24 February 2025

The reading largely deals with hypertext and its relationship to Marshall McLuhan’s Laws of Media. The article further explores the political, social, and cultural implications of this new form of media. With each of McLuhan’s four laws, Moulthrop makes it clear that the "revolution" is blocked by corporate-owned platforms and not truly fueled by intellectual engagement. This, however, seems to be the case with all media the world has ever encountered. Where there is media, there will be propaganda. Where there is information, it will be manipulated and withheld. Hypertext is not immune to this, and the influence seems to be greater since more and more information can be consumed faster, leading to misinformation and propaganda all around What does hypertext enhance? The first law of McLuhan’s Media is that a new form of media should enhance some sort of human faculty. The big enhancement of hypertext is its ability to closely simulate a human thought process. Nonlinear information retrieval and intertextuality skyrocketed. This is constantly improving and can be seen in activities like the Wikirace. What does hypertext render obsolete? Moulthrop states that hypertext has not rendered other media obsolete but has caused a significant shift in how people choose to engage with information. Many books have been converted to a hypertext format, where it is easy to jump from one chapter or topic to another. Hypertext has enhanced physical media and is slowly replacing it, with environmental concerns and convenience being big driving factors in today’s time. What does hypertext retrieve? Moulthrop states that hypertext is more interactive than books but less so than TV. He claims that hypertext has almost "fetched" old literacy and brought it into the interactive era. What happens when hypertext reaches its limit? McLuhan argued that when a medium is pushed to its extreme, it reverses into its opposite. Moulthrop applies this idea to hypertext, suggesting that rather than fostering an open, participatory network, it has increasingly been controlled by corporate and institutional interests. Links: wordstone.com

What does interaction mean to you?
Interaction, for the purposes of this course, has been defined as the ability to meaningfully change the state of something. This definition suits me well. I do, however, feel that this definition is geared towards excluding books and film. I see the value of this exclusion since the nature of these media is very linear and static. However, I believe that these forms of media can be included with an asterisk since the way in which you choose to interact with the media can change its influence on you, even if it does not truly change the media itself. Nonetheless, I think it is prudent to exclude these "static media" from our definition.

Blog 3: Website Planning

User Alignment and Mapping Out My Site.

3 March 2025

User Alignment: The primary goal of my portfolio website is to attract employment opportunities in technical fields such as Software Development, Game Design, or Web Development. My target audience consists of potential employers seeking candidates withtechnical skills and creative problem-solving abilities. To effectively engage these users, my portfolio must clearly communicate my expertise while providing a seamless and engaging browsing experience. Site Content Mapping: To organize my portfolio effectively, I have structured the content into different skill-based categories:

  • Software: Birds—a project showcasing my software development skills.
  • Game Design:
    • Games—a collection of games I have worked on.
    • Itch—a link to my Itch.io profile for game projects.
  • Personal Projects: Music—showcasing my personal creative projects outside of software and game development.
Each section will highlight my skills and accomplishments in the respective domain, making it easy for employers to quickly assess my capabilities. User Experience Focus: Employers often browse multiple portfolios when searching for candidates. To ensure my portfolio stands out, I aim to create an experience that is both visually appealing and functionally efficient. Key considerations include:
  1. Easy Navigation: Employers should be able to locate relevant sections without unnecessary clicks.
  2. Performance Optimization: A fast-loading website ensures a smooth and frustration-free experience.
  3. Intuitive Design: The site should not only look good but also feel good to use, providing an enjoyable interaction for the user.
Interface Elements: The interface design focuses on two primary aspects:
  • Aesthetics: Inspired by the DIY, collage-style aesthetic of zines, my portfolio will feature a creative and visually unique layout.
  • Functionality: Seamless navigation will enable users to explore content quickly while ensuring my skills and projects are clearly showcased.
The goal is to strike a balance between creativity and usability, making the site memorable while remaining professional and accessible. Development Progress Reflection: This week, my focus has been on familiarizing myself with HTML and starting to build the foundational structure of the site. While the current version is still rough, progress is being made towards refining the layout and content organization. One area needing more attention is the implementation of semantic HTML strategies. This will be my focus in the coming week as I work toward making the site more structured and accessible. By improving semantic markup, I aim to enhance both readability for search engines and usability for visitors.

Blog 4: IxD Process

My Interaction Design Process.

10 March 2025

Design Inspiration: I am drawing inspiration from classic zines, aiming to convey the aesthetic of print and physical media in my website. My focus is on incorporating this style while maintaining a functional and accessible design. Interaction Design Thoughts: My interaction design so far has prioritized navigation. I have added features like jump links and plan to integrate similar elements, such as status bars, to enhance usability. I anticipate that the interaction experience of the website will evolve, becoming more complex. My main concern is ensuring that I do not lose sight of my website's core purpose as I introduce additional elements. Ultimately, I want the site to communicate my skills in an accessible way that does not overwhelm or frustrate users. Wireframes Reflection: I feel my original wireframes have not been fully realized yet, which is why I believe they still hold up well. However, I expect they will be challenged as more stylistic and structural elements are added to the website. I see my wireframes as placeholders, serving to guide development and prevent me from working aimlessly. I hold no illusion that they represent the final iteration of my website. Instead, I fully expect them to evolve as the design progresses. Website 1 Development Reflection: The creation of Website 1 has been an interesting journey. While I have not found the implementation of languages particularly challenging, breaking down the design process into distinct layers rather than building component by component has been an adjustment. I often struggle with resisting the urge to add depth and complexity to the site prematurely, rather than broadening and refining it gradually. This has made it difficult to see my wireframes materialize both structurally and artistically. The disconnect between design and implementation, especially regarding structure, is a challenge I face—and one I believe others encounter as well. Semantic Markup and Microformatting: I find semantic HTML both fascinating and overwhelming. I frequently worry whether I am adding too much or too little, which can be exhausting. This struggle extends to microformatting, where similar concerns were even mentioned in the lecture videos. Despite this, I am proud of adhering to good semantic practices and avoiding hardcoding when organizing the structure or formatting. Key Accomplishments:

  • I am proud of the overall flow of my website, believing the navigation is simple, intuitive, and aligned with the website's purpose.
  • This focus on clear and accessible navigation is one of my core interaction principles, which I feel I have executed well.
Looking Ahead: My focus moving forward will be refining the layout, enhancing semantic markup, and continuing to align the design with the core principles of usability and accessibility.

Blog 9: Internet Access: A Right

A discussion on how Internet Access is a Fundamental Right.

21 April 2025
Internet Access is a Fundamental Right

This is a thought I’ve had for quite a while. It might seem idealistic or maybe even privileged, but it makes sense. I believe the following excerpt sums up the importance of this right: “Information and the access thereto, then, are two sides of the same coin, and few keep the currency.” The rich and powerful have always found ways to stay in control, whether ethically or not. Possessing what has value (for example land, capital, or knowledge) has historically equated to having power and status. In today’s world, information and education are among the most valuable things to possess and accessing them is next to impossible without the internet. Withholding information, or more specifically, the access to it, prevents people from advancing their “value.” And since the systems we live in require you to be “valuable” in order to survive, this withholding becomes almost akin to stripping someone of their right to live. I know how dramatic that may sound but considering the context of the pandemic (as discussed in the blog I’m referencing), it might not seem so far-fetched. During the pandemic, people without internet access were often unable to keep working, meaning their livelihoods were directly threatened. The digital divide has always been closely tied to the wealth gap, yet it doesn’t seem to receive the same level of attention or urgency.

Digital Inclusion

If people are not included, they will be left behind. The rich and powerful define what it means to be successful, and they often purposefully exclude those without access to resources. Technology is the latest manifestation of this cycle. The rapid pace of progress, accelerated by the internet, has made digital literacy a necessity. It’s become something we assume everyone should have, just like reading. But just as literacy was once deliberately withheld from the masses, access to technology and the internet is now limited by financial and infrastructural constraints. If governments don’t provide access to digital infrastructure, only the wealthy can participate in this new society. Think of it like transportation: if you own a car, you can take a job that’s further away. If the bus doesn’t reach your destination, you need a car. Internet access works the same way. If you don’t have it, you can’t reliably take on a remote job. Restricting access to the tools we use to define and drive progress only deepens inequality. I’m aware that all these systems require money to maintain, and I don’t claim to have all the answers. But I stand firm in believing that this is an issue we must take seriously and work to fix.

Blog 10: Close Reading: Internet Access: A Right

A Close Reading: "Internet Access is a Fundamental Right" by Spyros Kasapis.

28 April 2025

Spyros Kasapis’s blog post, “Internet Access is a Fundamental Right. Especially Amidst a Pandemic”, presents a compelling argument for digital inclusion, framed within the context of social inequality made clearer by the COVID-19 pandemic. The blog claims that internet access is not just a technological tool, but a necessity for participation in society. Kasapis equates internet access to human rights such as education and healthcare arguing that without it, millions are effectively locked out of modern life. Kasapis’s central aim is to reframe internet access as a fundamental human right, not a luxury. Sayin that institutions like the United Nations and Amnesty should act in ensuring access. He states that the Indian Supreme court has already taken such action. His aim is not only to raise awareness but to urge systemic responses, particularly from governments, to ensure digital access for all, especially considering crisis conditions like the pandemic.

He also introduces two larger questions for future discourse:

  1. Economic and social: Should internet access be universally guaranteed regardless of income?
  2. Political: Who governs the internet, and under what rules?

(These are the two questions posited with any change ever)

Kasapis uses a persuasive, socio-political lens to structure his blog. He begins with an evocative quote from Salvador Allende, framing happiness as a right obstructed by economic inequality, then pivots to extend this logic to digital access. He employs an appeal to authority (e.g., referencing international legal bodies) to ground his claims, while also using contrast to highlight disparities: Jeff Bezos’s success is juxtaposed with the digital exclusion of half the world. The piece moves in a cause-effect logic. Pandemic leads to a reliance on digital tools which leads to exclusion of those without internet which leads to widening inequality and so on. By doing so, Kasapis constructs a logical, morally urgent narrative that culminates in a call for governance, equity, and digital democracy. In my own blog response, I reflected on how digital exclusion mirrors earlier forms of systemic disenfranchisement, such as how literacy was once restricted to elites. The metaphor of transportation (“if a bus doesn’t go there, you need a car”) mirrors Kasapis’s logic of infrastructure: access begets access, and without it, you’re stuck. Kasapis’s arguments are supported by a growing body of research. The Alliance for Affordable Internet (A4AI) reports that nearly half the world’s population still lacks reliable access, citing affordability, infrastructure, and digital literacy as key barriers. Moreover, UNESCO has advocated for “meaningful connectivity”, not just access—emphasizing that internet quality, speed, and usage opportunities also matter. Scholars like Laura Mann and Nick Couldry have also written extensively on how platform capitalism creates new global hierarchies, where access determines one’s place in digital economies. Their work reinforces Kasapis’s view that the internet is not just a marketplace of ideas, but also labour and survival. Spyros Kasapis’s blog post is a concise yet powerful intervention into debates about digital inequality. By framing internet access as a human right deeply tied to survival, especially during times of crisis, he challenges readers to consider the ethical and political structures behind digital access. Through a mix of personal conviction, authoritative references, and global critique, he encourages a shift in how we perceive connectivity, not as a privilege, but as a foundational requirement for participation in society.

Blog 11: Feminist Internet

A Critical Reflection: "Making a Feminist Internet in Africa" by Sheena Magenya.

6 May 2025

The article Making a Feminist Internet in Africa: Why the Internet Needs African Feminists and Feminisms offers a timely and powerful meditation on the intersections between digital technology, access, and feminist activism on the African continent. Against the backdrop of the COVID-19 pandemic, it critiques the illusion of digital neutrality and challenges the idea that the internet inherently democratizes communication. Instead, the author positions African feminist movements as essential to resisting and reshaping an internet that reflects, existing inequalities. The central aim of the article is to make visible how access to the internet is uneven and deeply politicized, especially for women, LGBTIQA+ individuals, and other marginalized groups. The methodology is grounded in both personal narrative and collective experience, drawing on the October 2019 Making a Feminist Internet in Africa and the Diaspora gathering. This approach is significant because it reframes internet governance not as a top-down, policy-driven domain, but as a lived and shared concern for those often excluded from decision-making. One of the most compelling insights from the article is the critique of techno-solutionism, the belief that digital tools can solve complex social problems. This critique echoes broader concerns raised by scholars such as Evgeny Morozov and resonates with Spyros Kasapis’ blog post on internet access as a human right, which similarly highlights how structural inequalities shape digital participation. Both texts underscore that without considering context, infrastructure, and social positioning, digital “solutions” can exacerbate the very problems they claim to address. A recurring theme in the article is the “danger of a single story,” a concept popularized by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie. The author notes how African technological innovation is often framed through deficit narratives emphasizing survival rather than creativity, and necessity rather than joy. Feminist internet gatherings offer a counterpoint: spaces of joy, play, and shared knowledge that refuse reductive portrayals. These gatherings not only enable solidarity across borders but also challenge dominant digital aesthetics and content that marginalize Black, queer, fat, disabled, and non-normative bodies and identities. Importantly, the article situates the internet as both a tool of oppression and a site of resistance. It highlights how online gender-based violence is rampant, yet often unrecognized in African legal systems. This gap between online harm and offline redress illustrates the urgent need for feminist digital justice frameworks that can navigate the entanglement of online and offline realities. The author's call for a more inclusive, diverse, and joyful internet is not simply utopian, it’s strategic, rooted in lived experience, and attuned to both the risks and potential of the digital sphere. Ultimately, this article is not just a critique, but a call to action. It compels readers to imagine and work toward an internet where African feminists not only participate but lead. In a time of isolation and uncertainty, building such a digital space is not optional. It is, as the article states, “crucial.”

Essay 1: UI, UX and Ethics:

Essay on the UI and UX practices of the ratailer markro's website and its ethical implications.

21 April 2025
Interactive Media Essay UI, UX, and Ethics in the Makro Website
Introduction

User Interface (UI) refers to the point of interaction between humans and computers, encompassing visual and auditory elements such as typography, icons, buttons, animations, and sounds [1]. The design of a UI is closely intertwined with the User Experience (UX), which is the emotional and functional response users have while engaging with a system. While a conceptual distinction between UI and UX can be made, they are often best evaluated together in practice. In the contemporary digital landscape, websites are intentionally constructed to guide user interaction, often in service of commercial objectives. Profit-driven design is not inherently unethical; however, the strategies used to secure profit can tread the line between persuasive and manipulative. Furthermore, because legislation frequently lags behind technological innovation, defining ethical boundaries in digital design remains a complex and evolving challenge. This essay critically analyses the UI and UX design of the South African supermarket chain Makro’s website. It evaluates the site’s adherence to key interface principles, the psychological strategies underlying its user experience, and the ethical implications of its design decisions.

User Interface (UI)

According to core UI principles covered in the course, an effective interface should be intuitive, simple, and consistent. [7] An "intuitive" interface allows users to navigate without prior training, often achieved by adhering to established design conventions. "Simple" refers to a clean and manageable information layout, while "consistent" design ensures users do not need to relearn functions across pages. Makro’s website, as an e-commerce and catalogue platform, follows widely accepted web conventions. The homepage presents a broad array of products to inform users of available offerings. Features such as search, filtering options, and a shopping cart provide clear pathways to interaction and conversion. The site excels in consistency: product listings are uniformly structured, with transparent banner images, long-form names, pricing, and customer ratings. Although some product names are lengthy, this choice prioritizes informational clarity over minimalism. Filtering tools, categorized by price, brand, and rating, are predictably structured across sections, reducing cognitive load and supporting smooth user navigation. In terms of simplicity, the site employs a restrained colour palette (black text on a white background, with yellow accents), readable fonts, and universally understandable icons. Importantly, icons are always paired with text descriptions, reinforcing the emphasis on accessibility and clarity. However, the homepage lacks a strong visual hierarchy, a key UI principle defined by visual weight, contrast, spacing, and font size [2]. With numerous banners and categories vying for attention, users may struggle to prioritize content. The absence of contrast diminishes guidance, resulting in a page where all elements appear equally important, thus weakening overall navigability. In summary, Makro’s UI is largely intuitive, consistent, and user-friendly, though it would benefit from improved visual hierarchy to better guide user attention, specifically with the Home Page.

User Experience (UX)

UX design draws heavily on psychological principles intended to guide user behaviour. Notable among these are Hick’s Law, the Zeigarnik Effect, Fitts’s Law, and the Serial Position Effect [8]. According to other sources other relevant ideas include the active user Jakob’s Law. [3] Makro’s website demonstrates thoughtful application of these principles to enhance usability and encourage user engagement. Hick’s Law suggests that too many options can overwhelm users. While Makro’s homepage presents a wide selection of content, it does so in a well-structured layout that mitigates confusion. During the checkout process, unrelated options are removed, maintaining user focus and supporting conversion. The Zeigarnik Effect, which posits that incomplete tasks are more memorable than completed ones, is utilized through persistent “View All” and “Shop Now” prompts, encouraging continued engagement. The inclusion of a progress bar during checkout leverages this effect to nudge users toward finalizing purchases. Jakob’s Law highlights users’ preference for familiar interfaces. Makro adheres to established e-commerce conventions, allowing users to rely on prior knowledge. This aligns with the “active user” principle, which recognizes that most users learn by doing rather than reading manuals. In contrast, Fitts’s Law, which relates to the time required to move to a target area (i.e., clickable buttons), is less effectively implemented. On desktop, the category icons lead to dropdowns rather than direct navigation, adding unnecessary steps that hinder usability. More broadly, the UX reflects an understanding of the intended user, the general consumer. The site avoids polarizing aesthetics or statements, instead opting for neutrality and accessibility. It is clearly designed for wide appeal, aligning with Makro’s positioning as a mass-market retailer.

UI & UX Intersection

Makro’s interface and experience coalesce to support clear and frictionless navigation. Despite the size and variety of its catalogue, the website manages to maintain a relatively clean and structured layout. By prioritizing accurate product information and minimizing the influence of individual brand aesthetics, Makro maintains its identity as a neutral distributor. For example, although products like iPhones are sold, items often associated with status, the platform itself avoids adopting such branding cues. This deliberate neutrality reinforces the platform’s accessibility and functionality over prestige. The consistency of design supports predictability, while the subtle integration of persuasive UX strategies enhances usability without undermining user autonomy. The result is a platform that balances functionality, simplicity, and neutrality in service of its commercial goals. In summary, the design principles of both UI and UX are met but not exceeded. For example, the simplicity is not minimalism. All the relevant standards are addressed when needed but nothing more or less than what is needed is done.

Ethics

Ethical digital design requires both the inclusion of diverse, respectful content and the avoidance of manipulative practices. As defined by UX Primer, ethical UX prioritizes user autonomy, privacy, and well-being [4]. Makro’s website maintains an inclusive tone by avoiding targeted messaging and focusing primarily on product information. While the occasional marketing banner features diverse individuals, the general aesthetic remains universal and product-centred. This reinforces a broad, accessible appeal that does not alienate or overly target any demographic. Importantly, the site refrains from dark patterns, design tactics that deceive or coerce users. There are no false claims of urgency, limited availability, or misleading calls to action. Notifications are opt-in rather than disruptive, and users retain full control over their experience. Even transitions to third-party payment windows are clearly indicated, with easy navigation back to the main site. Respect for the user’s autonomy is clear. As noted in UX Matters, the distinction between persuasion and manipulation lies in the preservation of transparency and agency [5]. As stated previously, clear and correct information is a key component of the site. Transparency is present. Makro’s design choices clearly fall on the ethical side of this line. The site encourages purchases without obscuring information, misleading users, or restricting freedom of choice. The user does not have to create an account to browse or share their location if they don’t want to. Notifications are opt-in. Everything about the site is geared to not manipulating the user but simply guiding them. The user is able to opt out of a decision at any time and is informed when a decision will be final. There is further information available on return policies, delivery dates and more. Ethical design basically means making conscious design decisions that do good and do not harm anyone, either intentionally or unintentionally. The design of the site is intentional, and the intention is neutrality as a brand representation. Transparency of the process with regards to the product, price and more. Intentionality of respecting the user’s agency and choice. The website respects human rights, effort and experience as laid out in the Ethical Design Manifesto [6].

Conclusion

Makro’s website offers a compelling example of ethical, user-centred design in the context of e-commerce. Its interface is intuitive, consistent, and largely simple, though improvements in visual hierarchy would enhance clarity. The site applies psychological principles to guide users effectively through its vast product offerings without resorting to coercive or deceptive techniques. Importantly, its ethical approach respects user autonomy and promotes transparency. In achieving its commercial objectives, Makro manages to create a user experience that is both functional and principled. Makro’s design succeeds not because it is flashy or innovative, but because it is quietly functional, inclusive, and respectful. In a commercial world where manipulation is often normalized, this restraint is its most ethical feature.
