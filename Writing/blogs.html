Blog 1: GitHub and Goals ▾

My experience setting up GitHub and my goals for this course.

17 February 2025

Setting up GitHub was a straightforward process. I found it fascinating to discover how simple it is to publish a web page. This demystified the process of creating websites, for which I am grateful. I read “As We May Think at 65,” along with articles about the dot-com bubble and Web 2.0. These three readings complement each other well. One key takeaway for me was the personal aspect explored in both “Think 65” and “Web 2.0.” Bush envisioned access to information mimicking the human brain, aiming for a dynamic thought process rather than the hierarchical structure of alphabetized lists. I believe Hypermedia is the closest we've come to replicating the human process of jumping from one thought to another. The personal aspect, however, is what intrigues me the most. Web 2.0 focuses on making the web an interactive and personal experience. While this has many benefits, a concerning truth is that much of this personalization is profit-driven. Personalized ads are a prime example, though the implications go far beyond this. Main Concerns:

  1. Ecosystems of Conformity: Within the web, ecosystems are being created where people are shown only what they want to see and are reinforced in their beliefs. This could allow harmful views to thrive unchecked.
  2. Capitalist Overtones: The dot-com bubble illustrates how much of the web is driven by publicly traded companies. Their profits depend on continuous growth, which drives tactics aimed at capturing attention and increasing time spent online. Such practices can foster addiction and unsafe habits.

As a result, the web has become an overwhelming space. While Bush's vision of dynamic information retrieval has been achieved, it is often overshadowed by the noise created by profit-driven motives. This constant growth desired by tech companies seems unsustainable, and the dot-com bubble at the turn of the century signals the risks of unchecked expansion.

Blog 2: Laws of Media ▾

A reflection on Moulthrop's "Hypertext and the Laws of Media".

24 February 2025

The reading largely deals with hypertext and its relationship to Marshall McLuhan’s Laws of Media. The article further explores the political, social, and cultural implications of this new form of media. With each of McLuhan’s four laws, Moulthrop makes it clear that the "revolution" is blocked by corporate-owned platforms and not truly fueled by intellectual engagement. This, however, seems to be the case with all media the world has ever encountered. Where there is media, there will be propaganda. Where there is information, it will be manipulated and withheld. Hypertext is not immune to this, and the influence seems to be greater since more and more information can be consumed faster, leading to misinformation and propaganda all around What does hypertext enhance? The first law of McLuhan’s Media is that a new form of media should enhance some sort of human faculty. The big enhancement of hypertext is its ability to closely simulate a human thought process. Nonlinear information retrieval and intertextuality skyrocketed. This is constantly improving and can be seen in activities like the Wikirace. What does hypertext render obsolete? Moulthrop states that hypertext has not rendered other media obsolete but has caused a significant shift in how people choose to engage with information. Many books have been converted to a hypertext format, where it is easy to jump from one chapter or topic to another. Hypertext has enhanced physical media and is slowly replacing it, with environmental concerns and convenience being big driving factors in today’s time. What does hypertext retrieve? Moulthrop states that hypertext is more interactive than books but less so than TV. He claims that hypertext has almost "fetched" old literacy and brought it into the interactive era. What happens when hypertext reaches its limit? McLuhan argued that when a medium is pushed to its extreme, it reverses into its opposite. Moulthrop applies this idea to hypertext, suggesting that rather than fostering an open, participatory network, it has increasingly been controlled by corporate and institutional interests. Links: wordstone.com

What does interaction mean to you?

Interaction, for the purposes of this course, has been defined as the ability to meaningfully change the state of something. This definition suits me well. I do, however, feel that this definition is geared towards excluding books and film. I see the value of this exclusion since the nature of these media is very linear and static. However, I believe that these forms of media can be included with an asterisk since the way in which you choose to interact with the media can change its influence on you, even if it does not truly change the media itself. Nonetheless, I think it is prudent to exclude these "static media" from our definition.

Blog 9: Internet Access is a Right ▼

A discussion on how Internet Access is a Fundamental Right.

21 April 2025
Internet Access is a Fundamental Right

The two articles I’ve chosen to engage with are Tataki and Glynos’s “Digital Divide Widens” and Kasapis’s “Internet Access is a Fundamental Right.” Both pieces address a pressing and often overlooked reality: technology and internet access should not be luxuries reserved for the wealthy elite. The idea that internet access is a fundamental right is one I’ve believed in for some time. Some might view this as idealistic or even privileged, but I believe it is a reasonable and necessary stance in our digital age. As Kasapis states, “Information and the access thereto, then, are two sides of the same coin, and few keep the currency” [1]. This metaphor speaks volumes. Throughout history, the powerful have maintained control through exclusive ownership of valuable resources—land, capital, and, notably, knowledge. In today’s context, access to information and education holds similar weight. Without the internet, people are effectively locked out of these critical tools, limiting their social mobility and their perceived “value” in a system that equates worth with productivity and skill. Withholding internet access, therefore, is more than a logistical issue—it’s an ethical one. It can feel like denying someone the means to survive. This may sound dramatic, but the COVID-19 pandemic made the stakes very clear. As Tataki and Glynos point out, “The current pandemic has forced a transition online” [2]. For those without internet access, this shift meant the loss of jobs, education, and community.

Digital Inclusion

Digital inclusion is no longer optional. If people are not included, they will be left behind. According to UNESCO data cited by Tataki and Glynos, “Of all the students currently out of the classroom, 50%—or about 830 million—do not have access to a computer” [2]. These are “the most vulnerable among us,” and they’re being systematically excluded from the future. Their access to education (a right) is being taken away. In societies like ours, where digital literacy is as crucial as traditional literacy, being offline equates to being invisible. Historically, literacy was once reserved for the elite and intentionally denied to marginalised groups like slaves and women. Pihama and Lee-Morgan draw connections between colonisation, education, and the systematic denial of knowledge to Indigenous peoples [3]. The comparison is apt. Today, internet access plays a similar role in perpetuating exclusion. If governments don’t provide access to digital infrastructure, only the wealthy can participate in this new society. Think of it like transportation: if you own a car, you can take a job that’s further away. If the bus doesn’t reach your destination, you need a car. Internet access works the same way. If you don’t have it, you can’t reliably take on a remote job. The internet accelerates progress, but if access remains restricted by income and infrastructure, it will also accelerate inequality. I acknowledge that maintaining these systems requires resources. However, acknowledging complexity does not excuse inaction. This is a global issue with tangible, human consequences—and it deserves urgent attention and investment.

References
  • [1] S. Kasapis, *Internet Access is a Fundamental Right*. Our Cosmos, 19 May 2020.
  • [2] M. Tataki and D. Glynos, *Digital Divide Widens*. Our Cosmos, 10 May 2020.
  • [3] L. Pihama and J. Lee-Morgan, “Colonization, Education, and Indigenous Peoples,” in *Handbook of Indigenous Education*, Springer, 2019.

Blog 10: Close Reading: Internet Access: A Right ▼

A Close Reading: "Internet Access is a Fundamental Right" by Spyros Kasapis.

28 April 2025

Spyros Kasapis’s blog post, “Internet Access is a Fundamental Right. Especially Amidst a Pandemic,” presents a compelling case for digital inclusion by exposing how the COVID-19 pandemic illuminated deep-rooted inequalities in global internet access. Kasapis argues that internet connectivity is no longer a luxury—it is essential for meaningful participation in contemporary life. He equates digital access with basic human rights such as education and healthcare, stressing that without it, millions are effectively locked out of modern society [2]. The central aim of Kasapis’s piece is to reframe internet access as a fundamental human right. He calls on institutions such as the United Nations and Amnesty International to intervene, and notes that the Indian Supreme Court has already declared internet access a fundamental right. This move from commentary to advocacy demonstrates Kasapis’s intent not just to raise awareness, but to push for systemic, policy-level responses—particularly from governments tasked with safeguarding equality, especially during crises like the pandemic [2].

Kasapis raises two critical questions for future consideration:

  1. Economic and social: Should internet access be universally guaranteed regardless of income?
  2. Political: Who governs the internet, and under what rules?

Stylistically, Kasapis opens with a quote from Salvador Allende, framing happiness as a right constrained by systemic inequality, which he later connects to digital exclusion. He uses contrast effectively throughout the article—for example, highlighting Jeff Bezos’s wealth beside the fact that billions lack digital access. These rhetorical choices ground his argument in both moral urgency and socio-political critique [2].

The article is structured around a cause-and-effect framework:

  • The pandemic → heightened reliance on digital tools
  • Reliance on digital tools → exclusion of those without internet access
  • Exclusion → deepening structural inequality

Kasapis’s insights resonate with larger discussions of digital capitalism and inequality. In my own reflections on his work, I was reminded of how historical exclusion from literacy mirrors modern digital disenfranchisement. As I wrote previously, access begets access. If infrastructure (e.g., buses, schools, or networks) doesn’t reach you, you need an alternative (e.g., a car, private education, a computer)—and alternatives require money. A similar analogy appears in Timo Seidl’s recent work on digital capitalism, which critiques the illusion of universal access in gig economies. As Seidl writes, “There is an Uber for everything,” but participation still assumes access to the digital infrastructures enabling such services [1]. Without that infrastructure, you’re excluded not just from convenience—but from entire economic and social systems.

Kasapis’s arguments are also supported by major digital equity bodies. The Alliance for Affordable Internet (A4AI) estimates that nearly half the world’s population lacks reliable access, with affordability, infrastructure, and digital literacy being key barriers [3]. UNESCO has echoed these concerns, advocating not just for “access,” but for meaningful connectivity—emphasizing quality, reliability, and the ability to use the internet effectively in one’s daily life [4].

Kasapis concludes by reminding us that the internet is not just a marketplace of ideas, but of labour and survival. In our global digital economy, technology saves time, time enables income, and income enables survival—but access to technology is still a gatekeeper. This paradox is central to modern digital inequality [2].

In summary, Kasapis’s blog is a brief yet potent critique of the socio-political structures surrounding internet access. By framing connectivity as a human right essential to dignity and survival, he challenges us to rethink what inclusion truly means in the digital age. His argument is timely, persuasive, and crucial—particularly for policy makers, educators, and technologists tasked with building more equitable futures.

References
  • [1] T. Seidl, “Commodification and Disruption: Theorizing Digital Capitalism,” WI: Working Papers in the Humanities and Social Sciences, vol. 3, Jul. 2023.
  • [2] S. Kasapis, Internet Access is a Fundamental Right. Especially Amidst a Pandemic. Institute for Internet & the Just Society, May 2020.
  • [3] Alliance for Affordable Internet (A4AI), “The Affordability Report 2020.”
  • [4] UNESCO, “Global Education Monitoring Report: Technology in Education – A Tool on Whose Terms?” 2023.

Blog 11: Critical Reflection: Making a Feminist Internet in Africa ▼

A critical reading of the article "Making a Feminist Internet in Africa" and its implications for digital justice.

30 April 2025

The article Making a Feminist Internet in Africa: Why the Internet Needs African Feminists and Feminisms offers a timely and powerful meditation on the intersections between digital technology, access, and feminist activism on the African continent. Set against the backdrop of the COVID-19 pandemic, it critiques the illusion of digital neutrality and challenges the notion that the internet inherently democratizes communication. Instead, the authors position African feminist movements as essential to resisting and reshaping an internet that reflects existing inequalities [1].

The article highlights how access to the internet is deeply politicized, particularly for women, LGBTIQA+ individuals, and other marginalized communities. Drawing on the October 2019 “Making a Feminist Internet in Africa and the Diaspora” gathering, it blends personal narrative with collective experience to reframe internet governance as a lived concern rather than a purely institutional one [1].

A major insight is its critique of techno-solutionism—the belief that digital tools alone can fix complex social problems. This aligns with critiques by thinkers such as Morozov and resonates with Spyros Kasapis’s framing of internet access as a human right [2]. Both texts argue that without addressing structural inequalities, digital “solutions” often reinforce the very issues they aim to resolve.

The article also references Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie’s concept of the “danger of a single story” [3], challenging deficit-based narratives that frame African innovation as purely utilitarian. Instead, feminist internet spaces are shown to foster joy, creativity, and resistance. These gatherings confront dominant digital norms that marginalize Black, queer, fat, disabled, and other non-normative identities.

Importantly, the internet is depicted as both a tool of oppression and a site for resistance. The text details how online gender-based violence is prevalent but underrecognized in African legal contexts. This gap between digital harm and legal recourse underscores the need for feminist frameworks that bridge online and offline experiences [1].

Ultimately, the article is a call to action, advocating for an internet where African feminists lead—not just participate. In a time of increasing digital dependency and isolation, creating inclusive and just online spaces is not aspirational—it is, as the authors assert, “crucial” [1].

References
  • [1] Association for Progressive Communications (APC), Making a Feminist Internet in Africa: Why the Internet Needs African Feminists and Feminisms. 2019.
  • [2] S. Kasapis, Internet Access is a Fundamental Right. Especially Amidst a Pandemic. Institute for Internet & the Just Society, May 2020.
  • [3] C. N. Adichie, The Danger of a Single Story. TED Talk, Jul. 2009.

Blog 12: Decolonial Coding – Rethinking Interaction with JavaScript ▾

A reflection on using JavaScript to build more inclusive and culturally accessible web experiences.

23 May 2025

When thinking about how our code matters in the context of decolonialism, it’s important to remember that code is not neutral. The tools, languages, and frameworks we use and the assumptions we embed in our designs all come from specific worldviews. These worldviews often reflect dominant, Western norms. As a developer and designer, I’ve started to reflect on what this means for my own practice, particularly when creating web-based projects.

One of the key things I’ve become more aware of is accessibility; not just in terms of screen readers or keyboard navigation, but in cultural accessibility too. Who is my code speaking to? Who is left out? Am I designing with assumptions about language, internet speed, device capability, or even ways of understanding space and interaction? In a decolonial approach, I have to question whose needs are centered in my work and whether I am reinforcing systems of exclusion through my design choices.

This has influenced the way I approach interactivity, especially as I’ve started integrating JavaScript into my website. Initially, my site was a static HTML/CSS structure, which limited how users could interact with the content. Adding JavaScript forced me to rethink the layout and structure. I had to refactor parts of the site to allow for dynamic content updates, responsive feedback, and more intuitive navigation.

For example, I implemented a JavaScript-based menu that adapts to screen size and user input. Instead of relying on visual cues only, I began considering how to provide sound or text feedback for users who might interact differently, whether due to disability, device limitations, or different cognitive preferences. This small change became a gateway to bigger questions about inclusive interaction design.

Incorporating JavaScript also helped me streamline the user experience. I could now use event listeners and conditionals to guide users through tasks without overwhelming them with static content all at once. Forms became smarter. Feedback became immediate. But even here, I had to pause: was I relying too heavily on JavaScript in a way that made the site unusable for people on low-end devices or slow networks? Decolonial thinking reminded me that performance is not just a technical concern but it’s also a justice issue.

Ultimately, my development process is becoming less about “adding features” and more about asking better questions. JavaScript isn’t just a tool, it’s a medium that can either reinforce or resist dominant design norms. As a developer committed to ethical, inclusive practice, I want to use it to empower, not exclude.

This means staying critical of my own assumptions, seeking out alternative ways of thinking about interaction, and being intentional about whose voices and needs are considered in every line of code. That’s what decolonial coding looks like to me.

Blog 13: What Does an Ethical Internet Look Like to Me? ▼

Reflecting on ethics, design, inclusion, and power in digital space.

23 May 2025

Let’s imagine the internet as a meta-country—a nation without borders but with citizens, laws, infrastructure, and consequences. If that’s the case, then an ethical internet is a place I would want to live in: an environment conducive to good living, fairness, and well-being. It would embody the following principles:

  1. Inclusivity: An ethical internet is non-discriminatory—accessible and welcoming regardless of race, gender, disability, language, or location.
  2. Respect for User Rights: This includes control over one’s content, freedom of expression, and digital privacy.
  3. No Dark Patterns: Platforms should not rely on deceptive design to manipulate user behavior for profit.
  4. Consequences and Speed Bumps: Ethical design includes mechanisms that slow harmful behavior. Just like how speed bumps make you stop where speed limits don’t—especially for the wealthy or powerful who can ignore fines—ethical systems should include enforceable constraints, not just warnings.

An ethical internet, to me, is one where the rights, dignity, and safety of users come before profit, power, or unchecked surveillance. It’s not only about access to the internet, but also what kind of access, who controls it, and what values it reflects.

The article Making a Feminist Internet in Africa [1] shaped my thinking significantly. It argues that unless we consciously redesign digital spaces, the internet will reproduce—and even amplify—existing structural inequalities. A feminist internet is not just about women; it’s about challenging power imbalances, especially those rooted in patriarchy, colonialism, and racism. An ethical internet should amplify marginalized voices, not silence them through harassment or algorithmic bias. This requires platforms that are built with care and consent at their core.

Consent is a major pillar of ethical design. Shoshana Zuboff’s The Age of Surveillance Capitalism [2] describes how platforms like Facebook and TikTok extract and monetize data without meaningful consent, turning people into resources. Ethical alternatives must emphasize data transparency, user ownership, and robust regulation to prevent exploitative surveillance.

Accessibility is another key principle. An ethical internet ensures that people of all abilities, languages, and cultural backgrounds can participate fully. This includes features like screen reader compatibility, mobile-friendly design, multilingual content, and affordable access. This aligns with the concept of “universal design”, which centers the needs of the most vulnerable users as a standard rather than an afterthought.

Lastly, the internet should promote collective well-being. This doesn’t mean enforcing a single set of moral beliefs, but rather protecting pluralism while safeguarding users from harm. Platforms should be held accountable when their algorithms spread hate or misinformation. For instance, Zeynep Tufekci noted how YouTube’s recommendation system often pushes users toward radical content to maximize engagement, regardless of the consequences [3]. Ethical platforms would prioritize human rights over metrics.

In essence, an ethical internet is not just about better regulations or guidelines—it’s about reimagining digital space through the lens of care, equity, and justice. It should be shaped collaboratively, by everyone it affects: engineers, artists, educators, activists, and everyday users. That’s the kind of internet I want to help build.

References
  • [1] Association for Progressive Communications (APC), Making a Feminist Internet in Africa, 2019.
  • [2] S. Zuboff, The Age of Surveillance Capitalism, New York: PublicAffairs, 2019.
  • [3] Z. Tufekci, “YouTube, the Great Radicalizer,” The New York Times, Mar. 10, 2018.

Essay 1: UI, UX and Ethics ▼

Essay on the UI and UX practices of the retailer Makro’s website and its ethical implications.

21 April 2025

Introduction

User Interface (UI) refers to the point of interaction between humans and computers, encompassing visual and auditory elements such as typography, icons, buttons, animations, and sounds [1]. The design of a UI is closely intertwined with the User Experience (UX), which is the emotional and functional response users have while engaging with a system. While a conceptual distinction between UI and UX can be made, they are often best evaluated together in practice. In the contemporary digital landscape, websites are intentionally constructed to guide user interaction, often in service of commercial objectives. Profit-driven design is not inherently unethical; however, the strategies used to secure profit can tread the line between persuasive and manipulative. Furthermore, because legislation frequently lags behind technological innovation, defining ethical boundaries in digital design remains a complex and evolving challenge. This essay critically analyses the UI and UX design of the South African supermarket chain Makro’s website. It evaluates the site’s adherence to key interface principles, the psychological strategies underlying its user experience, and the ethical implications of its design decisions.

User Interface (UI)

According to core UI principles covered in the course, an effective interface should be intuitive, simple, and consistent [7]. An "intuitive" interface allows users to navigate without prior training, often achieved by adhering to established design conventions. "Simple" refers to a clean and manageable information layout, while "consistent" design ensures users do not need to relearn functions across pages. Makro’s website, as an e-commerce and catalogue platform, follows widely accepted web conventions. The homepage presents a broad array of products to inform users of available offerings. Features such as search, filtering options, and a shopping cart provide clear pathways to interaction and conversion. The site excels in consistency: product listings are uniformly structured, with transparent banner images, long-form names, pricing, and customer ratings. Although some product names are lengthy, this choice prioritizes informational clarity over minimalism. Filtering tools, categorized by price, brand, and rating, are predictably structured across sections, reducing cognitive load and supporting smooth user navigation. In terms of simplicity, the site employs a restrained colour palette (black text on a white background, with yellow accents), readable fonts, and universally understandable icons. Importantly, icons are always paired with text descriptions, reinforcing the emphasis on accessibility and clarity. However, the homepage lacks a strong visual hierarchy, a key UI principle defined by visual weight, contrast, spacing, and font size [2]. With numerous banners and categories vying for attention, users may struggle to prioritize content. The absence of contrast diminishes guidance, resulting in a page where all elements appear equally important, thus weakening overall navigability. In summary, Makro’s UI is largely intuitive, consistent, and user-friendly, though it would benefit from improved visual hierarchy to better guide user attention, specifically with the Home Page.

User Experience (UX)

UX design draws heavily on psychological principles intended to guide user behaviour. Notable among these are Hick’s Law, the Zeigarnik Effect, Fitts’s Law, and the Serial Position Effect [8]. According to other sources other relevant ideas include the active user Jakob’s Law [3]. Makro’s website demonstrates thoughtful application of these principles to enhance usability and encourage user engagement. Hick’s Law suggests that too many options can overwhelm users. While Makro’s homepage presents a wide selection of content, it does so in a well-structured layout that mitigates confusion. During the checkout process, unrelated options are removed, maintaining user focus and supporting conversion. The Zeigarnik Effect, which posits that incomplete tasks are more memorable than completed ones, is utilized through persistent “View All” and “Shop Now” prompts, encouraging continued engagement. The inclusion of a progress bar during checkout leverages this effect to nudge users toward finalizing purchases. Jakob’s Law highlights users’ preference for familiar interfaces. Makro adheres to established e-commerce conventions, allowing users to rely on prior knowledge. This aligns with the “active user” principle, which recognizes that most users learn by doing rather than reading manuals. In contrast, Fitts’s Law, which relates to the time required to move to a target area (i.e., clickable buttons), is less effectively implemented. On desktop, the category icons lead to dropdowns rather than direct navigation, adding unnecessary steps that hinder usability. More broadly, the UX reflects an understanding of the intended user, the general consumer. The site avoids polarizing aesthetics or statements, instead opting for neutrality and accessibility. It is clearly designed for wide appeal, aligning with Makro’s positioning as a mass-market retailer.

UI & UX Intersection

Makro’s interface and experience coalesce to support clear and frictionless navigation. Despite the size and variety of its catalogue, the website manages to maintain a relatively clean and structured layout. By prioritizing accurate product information and minimizing the influence of individual brand aesthetics, Makro maintains its identity as a neutral distributor. For example, although products like iPhones are sold, items often associated with status, the platform itself avoids adopting such branding cues. This deliberate neutrality reinforces the platform’s accessibility and functionality over prestige. The consistency of design supports predictability, while the subtle integration of persuasive UX strategies enhances usability without undermining user autonomy. The result is a platform that balances functionality, simplicity, and neutrality in service of its commercial goals. In summary, the design principles of both UI and UX are met but not exceeded. For example, the simplicity is not minimalism. All the relevant standards are addressed when needed but nothing more or less than what is needed is done.

Ethics

Ethical digital design requires both the inclusion of diverse, respectful content and the avoidance of manipulative practices. As defined by UX Primer, ethical UX prioritizes user autonomy, privacy, and well-being [4]. Makro’s website maintains an inclusive tone by avoiding targeted messaging and focusing primarily on product information. While the occasional marketing banner features diverse individuals, the general aesthetic remains universal and product-centred. This reinforces a broad, accessible appeal that does not alienate or overly target any demographic. Importantly, the site refrains from dark patterns, design tactics that deceive or coerce users. There are no false claims of urgency, limited availability, or misleading calls to action. Notifications are opt-in rather than disruptive, and users retain full control over their experience. Even transitions to third-party payment windows are clearly indicated, with easy navigation back to the main site. Respect for the user’s autonomy is clear. As noted in UX Matters, the distinction between persuasion and manipulation lies in the preservation of transparency and agency [5]. As stated previously, clear and correct information is a key component of the site. Transparency is present. Makro’s design choices clearly fall on the ethical side of this line. The site encourages purchases without obscuring information, misleading users, or restricting freedom of choice. The user does not have to create an account to browse or share their location if they don’t want to. Notifications are opt-in. Everything about the site is geared to not manipulating the user but simply guiding them. The user is able to opt out of a decision at any time and is informed when a decision will be final. There is further information available on return policies, delivery dates and more. Ethical design basically means making conscious design decisions that do good and do not harm anyone, either intentionally or unintentionally. The design of the site is intentional, and the intention is neutrality as a brand representation. Transparency of the process with regards to the product, price and more. Intentionality of respecting the user’s agency and choice. The website respects human rights, effort and experience as laid out in the Ethical Design Manifesto [6].

Conclusion

Makro’s website offers a compelling example of ethical, user-centred design in the context of e-commerce. Its interface is intuitive, consistent, and largely simple, though improvements in visual hierarchy would enhance clarity. The site applies psychological principles to guide users effectively through its vast product offerings without resorting to coercive or deceptive techniques. Importantly, its ethical approach respects user autonomy and promotes transparency. In achieving its commercial objectives, Makro manages to create a user experience that is both functional and principled. Makro’s design succeeds not because it is flashy or innovative, but because it is quietly functional, inclusive, and respectful. In a commercial world where manipulation is often normalized, this restraint is its most ethical feature.

Analysis 1: The Music of Outer Wilds. ▼

How the Music is Woven into the Essence of the Game.

14 May 2025

The essence of Outer Wilds is exploration for the sake of curiosity and connection. This reflection analyses how the music (distinct from the broader sound design) deeply supports and enhances these themes.

Everything Is Connected

The idea that everything is connected in Outer Wilds is central to its macro-level design and encourages the player to explore. This is exemplified by the ship’s log (above), a gameplay feature designed to help players piece together the overarching mystery. The music supports this sense of connection by being embedded into the core of the game’s design, rather than existing as a passive background layer. Composer Andrew Prahlow stated in an interview with Jesse Vitelli of Prima Games.com that he explicitly aimed to avoid creating “wallpaper” (Stuart, 2020) music that loops without intention, as is common in many games.

While numerous games feature memorable soundtracks—such as Hollow Knight or the Super Mario series—these tend to serve as complements to the world rather than components of it. Prahlow approached the Outer Wilds score as a film composer might: music is triggered only when it serves a narrative or emotional purpose. As a result, much of the game is spent in relative silence, with only the ambient sounds of the ship or the player’s spacesuit audible. This deliberate minimalism contributes to the game’s cinematic feel, even though it lacks traditional cutscenes, voice acting, or photorealistic visuals. While art direction plays a significant role, the music’s restraint and timing are equally critical to the game’s atmosphere.

This film-score approach supports the idea that Outer Wilds is intended to be a singular, impactful experience—not necessarily a game to be replayed or mastered, but one to be felt.

Homeliness and Vastness

As a lone traveller trapped in a time loop, the player explores a small solar system and uncovers the story of a lost civilization known as the Nomai. The game masterfully balances feelings of comfort and vastness—two contrasting sensations—and the music is integral to achieving this. Tracks like “Timber Hearth” and “Campfire Song” evoke warmth and familiarity, while pieces associated with the Nomai introduce tones that feel alien, ancient, and haunting. Prahlow, in an interview with Game Developer.com described “Timber Hearth” as grounded in a core idea of “relaxation,” and explained that the Nomai music was designed to sound like “a piano being ripped apart in space.” (Prahlow,2019)

The track “The Museum” blends motifs from both “Timber Hearth” and the Nomai themes—featuring synth drones, piano, and a banjo riff. It musically symbolizes the intersection of the Hearthians and the Nomai, reinforcing the game’s theme of cosmic connection. The game is often isolating. The music, or lack thereof, reinforces this solitude. When “End Times” begins to play, it marks a profound moment of reflection and acceptance as the universe resets once again.

Music and Mechanics

The game’s sense of isolation is not only countered by “End Times,” but also by the signalscope—perhaps the strongest argument for how the music is integrated into gameplay itself. At any moment, the player can pull out the signalscope and detect the musical signatures of fellow travellers playing their instruments. These diegetic songs remind the player that they are not truly alone. More than just a comfort, the signalscope is a mechanical tool for exploration, functioning as a musical compass that leads players to key locations.

Whenever the player enters an area of Nomai ruins, a piano motif begins to play. According to Prahlow, this was designed to “help you notice that you’re in a place of importance.” This subtle musical cue encourages exploration, reinforcing the central mechanic of uncovering knowledge. If the player becomes stuck, they can rely on the presence of these fragments of music to guide them. The mechanical design of the ship and jetpack is intuitive and polished, and the music complements these systems to amplify the joy of movement and discovery.

The Finale: A Symphony of Connection

The final sequence of Outer Wilds is emotionally profound and musically climactic. As the player exits the time loop by removing the warp core, “End Times” reprises—but this time, it is transformed. Each traveller is revealed to have been playing the same song all along, and together, they gather around a final campfire as the universe collapses, and a new Big Bang begins. This moment is underscored by a reimagined version of the main theme: now synthesized, expansive, and emotionally weighted. The music mirrors the narrative’s resolution—a blend of grounded intimacy and cosmic scope. Beginning with acoustic, folk-inspired melodies and evolving into something more ambient and otherworldly, the score encapsulates the lifespan of both civilizations and the emotional journey of the player.

Analysis 2: The Existentialism of Disco Elysium ▾

A critical reflection on how Disco Elysium expands the RPG genre.

14 May 2025

Disco Elysium stands apart as an RPG that dares to strip the genre to its philosophical roots. The game has truly simple mechanics—it's a point-and-click, largely text-based game, but beneath this minimalist surface lies a richly complex narrative structure. Rather than focusing on combat or loot, Disco Elysium leans heavily into narratology over ludology. In other words, it prioritizes story and character development over traditional gameplay systems.

will discuss how Disco Elysium fits comfortably within the confines of the RPG classification but challenges the stereotypes of the genre. According to Sam Liberty’s article RPG’s have the following defining features:

- You play as a specific, predefined character. - Character advancement/customization. - The character has game statistics. - The player makes decisions for the character.

All these requirements are met. The player plays as a washed-up detective with no memory of who he is. This may sound limiting compared to RPGs that let you create your own hero, but Disco Elysium turns that on its head. The game offers a unique kind of character customization: instead of upgrading weapons or armour, you are upgrading your own mind. Progression is deeply introspective. Your “skill tree” is really a thought cabinet, and your stats—Intellect, Psyche, Physique, and Motorics—are directly tied to your personality and how your character processes the world around him.

This is where Disco Elysium takes its boldest step. Your build doesn’t just influence what you do in the game—it shapes what you perceive. A highly empathetic character will notice things others miss in conversations, while one with high Inland Empire might start hallucinating. One early scene has the player examine the body of a hanged man—a central murder victim. If your character’s Inland Empire (a surreal, intuitive stat) is high enough, the corpse starts talking back. It’s not just a hallucination—it’s a moment that fuses character psychology with world interaction, blending narrative depth with mechanical consequence. Dialogue options shift dramatically depending on your stats.

One of Disco Elysium’s most striking mechanics is the internal thought system, where different parts of the protagonist’s psyche—like Authority, Empathy, or Inland Empire—interject during conversations, shaping dialogue choices and even how information is interpreted. This transforms typical RPG dialogue trees into a negotiation with yourself, tying mechanics directly into the game’s themes of identity and instability. This turns each playthrough into a fundamentally different experience, not because the world changes, but because your character’s lens on it does. This links to the philosophical nature of the game and the relativist idea that perception shapes reality. The player can continually choose to change their perception by doing drugs, putting skill points into other In this sense, the game feels infinite—not in terms of world size or quest quantity, but in how deeply it allows for roleplay and internal variance. The dragons you’re slaying aren’t monsters—they’re your own insecurities, delusions, and traumas.

Disco Elysium also draws inspiration from traditional tabletop RPGs (TTRPGs) rather than modern computer RPGs (CRPGs or ARPGs). The character sheet is clear and familiar, the game uses literal dice rolls for checks, and the art—expressionist, painterly—is evocative of analogue play. The game as a relatively small map, compared to other CRPGs like open world of Skyrim, and apots a topdown view, almost like looking at the board of a board game. This analogue aesthetic, paired with heavy textual storytelling, builds a tactile sense of immersion. It's familiar yet strange, like reading a novel with stats.

In Roger Caillois’ terms, Disco Elysium is squarely in the category of mimicry—the game is about adopting a role and fully inhabiting it. But this mimicry is deeply reflexive: by playing a character grappling with identity and meaning, you end up examining your own. The game’s absurdist realism and critical stance on politics, ethics, and society push you to engage not just with the fiction, but with real-world systems of power and marginalization. Ultimately, Disco Elysium is a philosophical RPG—a rare game that blends simple mechanics with existential weight. It doesn’t just let you play a character; it forces you to become one, flaws and all. And in doing so, it redefines what an RPG can be.

It shares some of the weaknesses common to narrative-heavy RPGs—especially in the midgame, where the sheer amount of lore, politics, and side content can feel overwhelming or even aimless. When you're just trying to find your gun, being pulled into dense ideological debates can feel frustrating. But this is also the point: Disco Elysium is not a power fantasy—it’s a vulnerability fantasy. By slowing down the pace and demanding intellectual engagement, it asks players to reflect rather than react. In doing so, it expands the RPG genre into something stranger, more honest, and deeply human.

Analysis 3: The Tutorializations of Super Mario Galaxy ▼

An analysis of how Super Mario Galaxy teaches players through intuitive design.

14 May 2025

Super Mario Galaxy: Teach It to Me Like I’m an 8-Year-Old

Super Mario Galaxy is a masterclass in tutorialization, especially in how it introduces new mechanics. Rather than using walls of text, it teaches players through level design, visual feedback, and interactive elements that feel natural and playful. This analysis breaks down those teaching techniques, with a focus on the tutorial and Freezeflame Galaxy.

The Tutorial Level: Learning Through Play

The game begins with a low-stakes challenge: catching three bunnies on a small spherical planet. It’s a fun and seemingly simple task, but it cleverly teaches core skills—movement, camera control, and navigation on non-Euclidean terrain—without any explicit instruction. Sutton-Smith’s concept of “frivolous play” fits well here: it’s enjoyable for its own sake, but it carries deep lessons.

The spherical planet subtly introduces players to a gravitational system that doesn’t follow the usual 3D platformer rules. By making the learning space playful and risk-free, the game fosters experimentation and comfort with new mechanics.

Shake It Up: Introducing the Launch Star

Shortly after, players encounter a Launch Star that requires shaking the Wii Remote—introducing motion controls as a central mechanic. This blends seamlessly into gameplay and is introduced in a low-pressure environment. Galaxy also uses minimal but effective text, often paired with icons or animations for clarity.

Each tutorial planet is small and contained. This avoids confusion and ensures each mechanic is tied to satisfying actions like launching or smashing crystals, reinforcing understanding through immediate feedback.

Visual Feedback and Camera Framing

The camera plays a major role in communicating feedback. For example, when vibrating obelisks are activated, the camera zooms out to show the entire effect before snapping back to Mario. This sequence shows cause and effect without words.

The obelisks’ visual similarity to breakable crystals also encourages experimentation. This kind of intuitive cueing helps players discover mechanics naturally—without explicit instruction.

Case Study: Freezeflame Galaxy

Later in the game, Freezeflame Galaxy demonstrates scaffolded learning with increasing complexity:

  • First Star: Introduces the Ice Flower. Players shake the Wii Remote to skate and then use the Ice Flower to freeze water into platforms. Environmental design gently forces players to engage with these mechanics.
  • Second Star: Introduces the Fire Flower. Players light torches to open gates, slightly increasing the difficulty and requiring more interpretation.
  • Third Star: Combines both mechanics. This is a final test of everything learned, reinforcing mastery through application.

The level design and progression ensure the player is never confused, while consistently learning and experimenting in new contexts.

Genre and Innovation

Galaxy is a 3D platformer at heart, but by leveraging new technology—like spherical level geometry and motion controls—it redefined what the genre could be. These shifts required strong tutorialization to bring players along for the ride. Its intuitive design was essential for making players feel empowered rather than overwhelmed.

The galactic theme supported this complexity, giving a narrative excuse for variety and physics-bending mechanics. The game expanded the genre without abandoning its roots.

Guidance Without Handholding

Even small features like level preview clips help orient players. The game respects players’ intelligence, trusting them to learn through curiosity and engagement. This is guidance—not handholding.

Conclusion: Super Mario Galaxy exemplifies how elegant tutorial design, visual communication, and playfulness can work together to guide players through novel mechanics without overwhelming them. It creates a sense of discovery, not instruction—and that’s what makes it brilliant.
